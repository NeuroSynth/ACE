Abstract

In this study, functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) was used to examine the brain activation and connectivity in occipitotemporal cortex during Chinese character recognition (CCR). Eighteen healthy participants were recruited to perform a well-designed task with three categories of stimuli (real characters, pseudo characters, and checkerboards). By inspecting the brain activation difference and its relationship with behavioral data, the left laterality during CCR was clearly identified in the Brodmann area (BA) 18 and 19. In addition, our novel findings also demonstrated that the bilateral superior temporal gyrus (STG), bilateral BA 19, and left fusiform gyrus were also involved in high-level lexical information processing such as semantic and phonological ones. Meanwhile, by examining functional brain networks, we discovered that the right BA 19 exhibited enhanced brain connectivity. In particular, the connectivity in the right fusiform gyrus, right BA 19, and left STG showed significant correlation with the performance of CCR. Consequently, the combination of fNIRS technique with functional network analysis paves a new avenue for improved understanding of the cognitive mechanism underlying CCR.

Introduction

The word/character recognition is characterized by the process of three cognitive components: orthographic processing, semantic processing, and phonological processing (Wu et al. 2012). To date, various models have been proposed to examine the complex cognitive mechanism underlying word/character recognition. For example, Coltheart et al. (2001) presented a dual route cascaded (DRC) model to describe lexical processing in English and other alphabetic languages. This DRC model demonstrates that there exist two parallel routes in word recognition: the lexical route in which an orthographic form is matched to the mental lexicon, and the sub-lexical route in which a word is accessed through grapheme-phoneme conversion. In particular, for the sub-lexical route, the phonology of a word can be retrieved without the complete recognition of the orthographic form (Coltheart et al. 2001). Meanwhile, a lexical constituency model was also developed for Chinese character recognition (CCR), which indicates that the orthographic form is an essential step to retrieve a Chinese character’s phonological and semantic information (Perfetti et al. 2005).

Although behavioral studies have been well performed to inspect the models mentioned above, it is insufficient for them to unveil the complex cognitive processing and possible neural mechanism associated with CCR. By contrast, the functional neuroimaging techniques such as functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), electroencephalograph(EEG)/event-related potential (ERP), and functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) have made themselves ideal candidates for clinical investigations of brain activation patterns and brain connectivity related to CCR. For example, previous fMRI studies demonstrated that the left lateral occipitotemporal region was consistently activated during the processing of “Visual Word Form” of either an alphabetic word or a Chinese character (Cohen et al. 2000; Dehaene et al. 2002; Bolger et al. 2005; Dehaene and Cohen 2011; Krafnick et al. 2016). Interestingly, besides the identified “Visual Word Form Area” (VWFA), the right occipitotemporal region was also activated during processing the Chinese characters (Bolger et al. 2005; Tan et al. 2005). In addition, fMRI studies also suggested that the brain activation in the right STG, left BA 19, and left inferior parietal lobule showed the correlation with Chinese phonological processing (Dietz et al. 2005), whereas the left middle temporal gyrus and left STG were involved in semantic processing (McDermott et al. 2003; Chou et al. 2006; Wu et al. 2012). Likewise, a variety of ERP components have been identified for CCR, in which the N170 component is recognized to be associated with orthographic processing (Bentin et al. 1999), whereas the N400 component is related to semantic processing (Kutas and Federmeier 2011). In addition, the brain region associated with N170 component were identified in the bilateral occipital cortex (Liu and Perfetti 2003; Hsiao et al. 2007; Lee et al. 2007; Xue et al. 2008). Further, the centro-parietal cortex N200 component is more specific to Chinese visual word recognition (Zhang et al. 2012), which is due to the involvement of more holistic and complex analysis of spatial information (Tan et al. 2001; Liu and Perfetti 2003; Bolger et al. 2005). Compared to fMRI and EEG/ERP, fNIRS offers quantitative information for both oxyhemoglobin (HbO) and deoxyhemoglobin (HbR), which is essential for revealing the rapid neurovascular changes of the brain. More importantly, fNIRS can be operated in a portable, comfortable, and quiet way with fewer body constraints, and the operating costs are much lower than fMRI (Yuan and Ye 2013; Yuan 2013, 2014). Although fNIRS has been increasingly utilized in language studies (Dieler et al. 2012; Rossi et al. 2012; Ferrari and Quaresima 2012), only very limited studies were carried out to inspect the processing of Chinese characters. Moreover, the fNIRS studies completed mainly focused on revealing the brain activation patterns related to phonological processing by examining brain activities in the frontal lobe including Broca’s area (Gan et al. 2003; Zhang et al. 2006; Oi et al. 2010; Sun et al. 2011). To the best of our knowledge, the brain activation and local networks in occipitotemporal cortex (OTC), which also plays an essential role in CCR (Bolger et al. 2005; Tan et al. 2005; Dehaene and Cohen 2011), has not been explored by fNIRS. In particular, the network property of brain functional connectivity associated with CCR remains obscure.

In this study, fNIRS is used to examine the functional brain activation and connectivity in OTC during CCR. It is hypothesized that brain activation and networks should exhibit significant differences between the non-orthography processing, orthography processing, and high-level lexical information processing such as phonological and semantic information processing. To test the hypothesis, the visual lexical decision paradigm (Dehaene and Cohen 2011) was adopted for the present work, which consists of three categories of stimuli including checkerboards, pseudo characters, and real characters. According to the lexical constituency model (Perfetti et al. 2005), checkerboards contain no orthographic information, whereas pseudo characters involve processing orthographic information without touching upon phonological and semantic information, and real characters implicate orthographic, phonological and semantic information. In addition, we compared and inspected the differences in brain functional activation and connectivity between the orthography and checkerboard cases, and also between the real character and pseudo character cases. Further, we correlated the differences in brain activation and connectivity between the real character and pseudo character cases with the differences in behavior data including mean accuracy and mean reaction time (RT) from each participant. It is expected that the investigation into the brain activation and connectivity during CCR will help establish a new method for improving the understanding of the neural mechanism of CCR in a comprehensive way.

Methods and Materials

Participants

Eighteen participants (10 females, eight males) participated in this study. The age range was from 20 to 32 years (mean age: 25.3 years). All participants were college students, who were right-handed, native Chinese Mandarin speakers, and had normal or corrected-to-normal vision. All participants were required to sign informed consent documents prior to the experiment. This study was approved by the Medical Ethics Committee with the University of Macau.

Tasks and Materials

The paradigm for the present study contained three categories of stimuli: (1) Chinese characters (real characters); (2) pseudo characters; and (3) non-character checkerboards. Each of the three categories of stimuli consisted of 48 different items, and all the 144 items were presented randomly. Real characters were commonly used ones with the mean frequency of 450.94 per million words. The frequencies of real characters were calculated according to a database based on film subtitles (Cai and Brysbaert 2010). By contrast, pseudo characters were artificially produced by using combined radicals from real characters. More importantly, the components of pseudo characters were placed in appropriate configurations, following the same orthographic rules as real characters. It is noted here that pseudo characters were orthographically legal without having lexical representation. The only difference between the pseudo and real characters was that pseudo ones were meaningless and unpronounceable. However, the stimulus set of checkerboards was neither orthographically legal nor of lexical representation.

Procedures

Participants were seated comfortably in a quiet room with about 70 cm away from the computer monitor (Fig. 1). Visual lexical decision tasks were performed, in which participants were instructed to determine whether a stimulus was a Chinese character or not, by pressing specific keys with their left or right forefinger. The response hands were counterbalanced across participants. After a brief practice, participants were requested to perform the visual lexical decision task with three runs. Each run included 48 trials and the duration of each trial was 15.5 s, which included a pre-stimulus period of 500 ms with a black fixation cross presented in the center of the monitor, a stimulus period of 2000 ms with the first 1000 ms severed as the response window, and then a post-stimulus and recovery period of 13 s with a red fixation cross displayed in the center of the monitor (Fig. 2). It took about 40 min to finish the experiment. Participants were allowed to have a short break between different runs, the duration of which could be determined by each individual participant.

fNIRS Data Acquisition and Preprocessing

A continuous wave (CW) fNIRS system (CW6 fNIRS system; TechEn Inc., Milford, MA) was used to measure the concentration changes of HbO and HbR. This system has four laser sources at wavelengths of 690 and 830 nm and eight optical detectors, and the sampling rate of this system is 50 Hz. The optodes were positioned on each participant’s head with two home-made plastic patches (4.24 cm × 8.49 cm), which can cover the occipitotemporal gyrus (Fig. 1). The configuration of the source and detector pairs can generate 12 channels and the inter-optodes distance was 3 cm. A three-dimensional (3D) digitizer (PATRIOT, Polhemus, Colchester, Vermont, USA) was used to measure the 3D coordinates of the optodes. The average 3D coordinates were further imported to NIRS_SPM software for spatial registration (Ye et al. 2009), which can generate the distributions of the optodes and channels (Fig. 3) and the MNI coordinates of each channel (Lancaster et al. 2000). The spatial information of each channel was provided in Table 1.

fNIRS signals were processed by using HomER2 software (Yang et al. 2010). The raw intensity data were first converted to the optical density changes. The motion artifacts were then corrected using the Spline interpolation algorithm (Scholkmann et al. 2010). After motion artifacts correction, the optical density changes were further processed by using a low cut off filter of 0.01 Hz and subsequently by a high cut off filter of 0.3 Hz. After band path filter, the optical density changes were converted to the concentration changes of HbO and HbR at different time points by using the Beer–Lambert law. Finally, concentration changes were normalized to zero mean and unit variance (z-scores) channel by channel(Zhang et al. 2010; Ding et al. 2013, 2014; van de Rijt et al. 2016). After the preprocessing, the run averages of the HbO and HbR data were calculated for each channel, and the grand averages were also performed for all participants underlying the three conditions. To distinguish well between the brain activation of checkerboard condition and other language-related conditions, the time courses of the real character and pseudo character cases were merged to generate the data of so-called orthography condition.

In this study, only HbO signals were analyzed, since it is a more sensitive indicator of changes in regional cerebral blood flow (Strangman et al. 2002; Fu et al. 2014). With the first 2 s data served as the baseline before the trigger onset, the mean z-scores during the time course from − 2 to 13.5 s were generated to denote the brain activation in corresponding channels.

Network Construction and Analysis

The nodes and edges are the two essential components to construct the brain networks. In this study, the node is denoted as the channel while the edge is defined as the Pearson correlation coefficient calculated from measurements between two channels. All correlation coefficients were then transformed into z-scores with the Fisher transformation. All the z-scores were averaged across participants for each condition, which were then converted back to correlation coefficients again.

To construct the brain network, the correction coefficients were binarized by setting a threshold T, in which the correlation matrix was converted into a binary undirected graph G by using the following graph construction:

$${e_{ij}}\;=\;\left\{ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {1,}&{if\left| {{r_{ij}}} \right| \ge T} \\ {0,}&{otherwise} \end{array}} \right.,$$
(1)

If the absolute rij is larger than a given threshold T, the connection between two nodes is set to 1 and the edge is kept, otherwise the edges can be seen as non-existent in the network.

The nodal degree was also computed to measure the significance of a node in the brain networks (Rubinov and Sporns 2010). The nodal degree of a node is denoted as the number of edges directly linked to the node. Since there is presently no unified standard for threshold selection (Zhang et al. 2016), this study used the whole correlation coefficient range of 0 < r ≤ 1 (interval = 0.01) as the thresholds. The correlation coefficients higher than a series of thresholds were visualized in binary maps for each condition.

Statistical Analysis

To analyze the behavioral data, the accuracy of the responses and the RT for correct responses was calculated for each participant. The accuracy and RT were subjected to a 3 × 1 repeated measures analysis of variance (ANOVA), respectively, with the stimuli type as a within-subject factor. Subsequently, Post-hoc tests were performed with Sidak adjustment.

In this study, the HbO signals and nodal degree differences between the orthographically legal stimuli and checkerboards cases, and the differences between the pseudo and real character cases during high-level lexical information processing were carefully examined, respectively. Two-tailed paired t-tests were also performed on the HbO signals and nodal degree for each channel.

Further, we correlated the difference in HbO signals/nodal degree between the real character and pseudo character cases with the difference in the mean accuracy/RT between the two cases. The significance of the correlation was also carefully inspected.

Results

Behavioral Results

We discovered that the stimuli type exhibited main effect on RT (F(2,34) = 81.33, p < 0.001, partial η2 = 0.83) and accuracy (F(2,34) = 27.67, p < 0.001, partial η2 = 0.62). In addition, the post-hoc tests (Sidak adjustment) were also performed and the analysis results were provided in Fig. 4, in which any two of the three conditions (checker, real, and pseudo stimuli cases) exhibited significant difference in RT and accuracy.

Brain Activation

The time courses of mean HbO signals (z-scores) were depicted in Figs. 5 and 6. The t-values, resulted from two contrasts (the HbO signal difference between the orthographically legal stimuli and checkerboards cases, and the difference between the pseudo and real character cases), were visualized in Fig. 7 by using xjView toolbox (http://www.alivelearn.net/xjview) and BrainNet Viewer toolbox (http://www.nitrc.org/projects/bnv/) (Xia et al. 2013), and the detailed t-test results were listed in Table 2.

The statistical analysis results for brain activation associated with the two conditions (orthography to checker; real to pseudo) were given in Table 2. The left laterality was clearly identified for the contrast case between orthography and checker conditions. For example, four out of the six channels (channels 9–12) on the left hemisphere showed the positive t values, whereas all channels on right hemisphere exhibited the negative ones, especially for the channel 2 (right STG, t(17) = − 2.59, p = 0.02, Cohen’s d = − 0.40) and channel 5 (right BA 19, t(17) = − 2.81, p = 0.01, Cohen’s d = − 0.42). Although the left hemisphere didn’t exhibit significant activation, the asymmetric activation pattern was clearly identified for the contrast case of orthography to checker (Fig. 7a; Table 2). Importantly, a paired t-test was also performed to examine the brain activation for each pair of symmetric channels in bilateral OTC. We discovered that for the six pairs of channels, the brain activation in the left hemisphere were higher than that from the right hemisphere, as shown in Table 3. In particular, BA 18 and BA 19 exhibited the significant activation at p < 0.05.

Further, compared to the pseudo character case, the real character case also elicited enhanced brain activation for almost all channels except the right fusiform gyrus (FFG). Even though the statistical results were not significant, the tendency was obvious (Fig. 7b; Table 2).

Relationship Between the Brain Activation and Behavioral Performance

The behavioral data for each participant’s performance including RT and accuracy were provided in Fig. 8. It was discovered from Fig. 8 that most participants exhibited lower performance in accuracy (with two exceptions) and RT (with one exception) for the pseudo character case as compared to that from the real character case. We then inspected the relationship between the relative change of behavioral performance and relative brain activation for the pseudo character case. It was revealed that the relative change of accuracy showed significantly positive correlation with the relative activation evoked by pseudo characters for a large number of channels in the bilateral OTC, including the bilateral STG, bilateral BA 19 (channels 5, 6, 9, and 11), and left fusiform gyrus. Meanwhile, the relative activation evoked by pseudo characters in the left STG exhibited negatively correlation with the relative change of RT. The correlation coefficients and corresponding p values were displayed in Fig. 9.

Brain Network Analysis

The grand-averaged correlation coefficients for each condition were visualized in matrix forms in Fig. 10. Subsequently, the t-values, resulted from two contrasts (the connectivity difference between the orthographically legal stimuli and checkerboards cases, and the difference between the pseudo and real character cases) were also visualized in matrix forms in Fig. 11, with the significant results highlighted with white squares. We discovered from Fig. 11 that compared to the checkerboard case, the orthography case showed increased functional connectivity between the right BA 19 (channel 3) and bilateral fusiform gyrus, and left BA 19 (channel 11). Meanwhile, compared to the pseudo character case, the real character case exhibited enhanced connectivity between different regions in the right BA 19 (channels 3 and 6).

The binary maps were generated with the thresholds ranged from 0.86 to 0.95. Figure 12 displayed the constructed brain networks with various thresholds, whereas Fig. 13 visualized the connectivity with thresholds higher than 0.95 by using BrainNet Viewer toolbox, in which the orthography condition case exhibited increased functional connectivity in the right BA 19 (channel 3). The binary maps implied that the right BA 19 (channel 3) played an essential role in the perception of orthography stimuli.

Consistent with the findings revealed by binary maps, increased nodal degree in the right BA 19 (channel 3) for the orthography condition (t(17) = 2.46, p = 0.02, Cohen’s d = 0.34) was identified.

Relationship Between the Brain Network Efficiency and Behavioral Performance

The relative change of nodal degree evoked by pseudo characters in the right fusiform gyrus and right BA 19 (channel 3) showed positive correlation with the relative change of accuracy (Fig. 14a, b). Meanwhile, the relative change of nodal degree in the left STG exhibited negative correlation with the relative change of RT (Fig. 14c).

Discussion

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first fNIRS study to explore the functions of OTC in CCR. In particular, the present study examined the differences in behavioral performance, brain activation, and functional brain connectivity between the orthographic processing and high-level lexical information processing such as phonological and semantic processing.

Interestingly, the behavioral results showed that the RT was much faster and the accuracy was much higher for the checkerboard stimuli as compared to those from the pseudo and real character cases. It is due to the fact that the checkerboard case does not satisfy the orthographic configuration of Chinese characters and never involves orthographic, phonological, or semantic processing. By contrast, the pseudo character case is orthographically legal, which is hard to be discriminated from the real character case. As a result, the RT was slower in response to the pseudo characters as compared to that in response to the real characters.

More importantly, we successfully identified the difference in brain activation between various cognitive processing in CCR. Firstly, the asymmetric activation pattern during CCR (Bolger et al. 2005) was clearly identified. In particular, we discovered that the left BA18 and left BA 19 exhibited higher activation than the right analogous region. Meanwhile, previous studies have revealed that the left BA 18 was involved in visual word form processing (Vorobyev et al. 2004), whereas the functions of left BA 19 were related to phonological processing (Dietz et al. 2005). As such, the previous findings showed good agreement with our results. In addition, we also discovered that the real character case elicited higher brain activation for most of the channels compared to the pseudo character case. Further, multiple brain regions including the bilateral STG, bilateral BA 19, and left fusiform gyrus exhibited positive correlation between the relative brain activation and relative accuracy changes for pseudo character case. The correlation results indicated that increased activation in these regions, which were reported to be associated with the process of high level lexical information, enhanced the discrimination of pseudo characters from real characters. Likewise, the relative activation in left STG showed negative correlation with the relative changes of RT, suggesting that the engagement of semantic processing may promote the discrimination of pseudo characters.

In addition, we also examined the difference in brain connectivity between various cognitive processing in CCR. The binary map visualized in Fig. 13 implied that the right BA 19 played an essential role in the perception of orthography stimuli. We also discovered that networks associated with the right BA 19, bilateral fusiform gyrus, and left BA19 were synchronously involved in the recognition of orthography stimuli. Our findings also showed that the real character case exhibited larger connectivity strength in the right BA 19 than the pseudo character case. Specifically, the orthography condition also showed a higher nodal degree in the right BA 19 compared to the checkerboard case. Importantly, nodal degree changes in pseudo character condition exhibited significant relationship with participants’ behavioral performance, which was demonstrated by the positive correlation between the relative nodal degree in right BA 19 and the relative accuracy, and by the negative correlation between the relative nodal degree in left STG and the relative accuracy. Altogether, it is suggested that the right BA 19 might be the key hub in the brain network associated with the CCR.

In summary, the present study successfully mapped the hemodynamic responses and brain connectivity in OTC during CCR by using fNIRS recordings. Our results demonstrated that the behavioral data, and brain activation/ functional networks exhibited significant differences during different cognitive processing associated with CCR. Compared to previous fMRI or EEG reports, the roles of OCT were carefully inspected in an integrative way by using task-evoked fNIRS recordings. In particular, the functions of key hub of right BA 19 was examined, which exhibited significant correlation with CCR. Our neuroimaging results also suggested that fNIRS is a robust tool for the investigation of neural mechanism underlying Chinese language processing. Combined with its advantages in portability, comfort, and cheap operating costs, the fNIRS technique would be among the best candidates for the studies of the CCR on a large sample of children, which can reveal the neural development underlying CCR.

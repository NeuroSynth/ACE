Abstract

Objective

Cortical tremor/myoclonus is the hallmark feature of benign adult familial myoclonic epilepsy (BAFME), the mechanism of which remains elusive. A hypothesis is that a defective control in the preexisting cerebellar-motor loop drives cortical tremor. Meanwhile, the basal ganglia system might also participate in BAFME. This study aimed to discover the structural basis of cortical tremor/myoclonus in BAFME.

Methods

Nineteen patients with BAFME type 1 (BAFME1) and 30 matched healthy controls underwent T1-weighted and diffusion tensor imaging scans. FreeSurfer and spatially unbiased infratentorial template (SUIT) toolboxes were utilized to assess the motor cortex and the cerebellum. Probabilistic tractography was generated for two fibers to test the hypothesis: the dentato-thalamo-(M1) (primary motor cortex) and globus pallidus internus (GPi)-thalamic projections. Average fractional anisotropy (FA), axial diffusivity (AD), mean diffusivity (MD), and radial diffusivity (RD) of each tract were extracted.

Results

Cerebellar atrophy and dentate nucleus alteration were observed in the patients. In addition, patients with BAFME1 exhibited reduced AD and FA in the left and right dentato-thalamo-M1 nondecussating fibers, respectively false discovery rate (FDR) correction q < .05. Cerebellar projections showed negative correlations with somatosensory-evoked potential P25-N33 amplitude and were independent of disease duration and medication. BAFME1 patients also had increased FA and decreased MD in the left GPi-thalamic projection. Higher FA and lower RD in the right GPi-thalamic projection were also observed (FDR q < .05).

Significance

The present findings support the hypothesis that the cerebello-thalamo-M1 loop might be the structural basis of cortical tremor in BAFME1. The basal ganglia system also participates in BAFME1 and probably serves a regulatory role.

Key points

  • Benign adult familial myoclonic epilepsy is a network disorder involving the cerebello-cortical circuit and basal ganglion loop.
  • Structural abnormalities in the cerebello-thalamo-M1 (primary motor cortex) loop might be the foundation of cortical tremor.
  • The globus pallidus internus (GPi)-thalamus projection also participates in benign adult familial myoclonic epilepsy and probably serves a regulatory role.

1 INTRODUCTION

Benign adult familial myoclonic epilepsy (BAFME)—also known as familial cortical myoclonic tremor and epilepsy, familial adult myoclonic epilepsy, and autosomal-dominant cortical myoclonus and epilepsy—is a rare epileptic syndrome that has not yet been officially recognized.1 Major clinical symptoms of BAFME include cortical tremor/myoclonus and infrequent generalized tonic–clonic seizures.1 Recently, noncoding repeat expansions in different genes, including pentanucleotide repeats in the SAMD12 (BAFME1), STARD7 (BAFME2), MARCH6 (BAFME3), YEATS2 (BAFME4), TNRC6A (BAFME6), and RAPGEF2 (BAFME7), have been recognized to play an essential role in BAFME.2

Owing to the high expression of BAFME-related genes in the cerebellum,2 pathological findings,3, 4 and abnormal cerebellar structure and function detected through multimodal imaging,5-13 the cerebellum appears to play a key role in BAFME. However, clinically, BAFME is characterized by cortical tremor and myoclonus with giant somatosensory-evoked potential (SEP) and long-latency cortical reflex, suggesting that the sensorimotor cortex actively drives the cortical tremor/myoclonus.14 The inconsistency between the cortical hyperexcitability and the cerebellum pathology raises the hypothesis stated by Latorre et al. that a defective control in the preexisting cerebello-cortical loop might induce the disinhibition within the primary motor cortex (M1), which combines with abnormal cortical transmission and result in cortical tremors.2 The dentato-thalamo-M1 tract has been considered the main cerebellar-cortical component,15, 16 which travels from the cerebellar dentate nucleus through ipsilateral superior cerebellar peduncle and consists of two components: most fibers decussate and traverse through the ventral thalamus and terminate in the contralateral M1, leaving a small portion of nondecussating fibers.17, 18 However, whether the dentato-thalamo-M1 structural abnormality is the foundation of cortical tremor remains unclear. Probabilistic tractography, a noninvasive method capable of reconstructing more anatomically plausible fiber tracts, is necessary to decipher the neural connectivity and anatomic basis of the disease.

In addition, case reports with positron emission tomography (PET) have suggested that the basal ganglia is involved in BAFME.12, 19 Recent evidence provided a perspective that the cerebellar-cortical and the basal ganglia networks not only communicate at the level of the cerebral cortex, but are also densely interconnected in subcortical regions to form an integrated network.20 Within the basal ganglia loop, the globus pallidus internus (GPi) constitutes the main output structure and sends efferences to the thalamus.21 However, the role of the GPi-thalamic tract in BAFME has not been explored.

The main objective of the present study was to test the structural foundation of the preexisting cerebello-cortical loop hypothesis and investigate the structural alterations of the basal ganglia loop in BAFME. Using both T1 and diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) scans, we compared anatomic changes in the motor cortex, cerebellum, and two primary fiber tracts (dentato-thalamo-M1 and GPi-thalamic tracts) in Chinese BAFME type 1 (BAFME1) pedigrees.

2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

Subjects

The study included 19 patients with BAFME1 and 30 demographically matched healthy controls from two imaging centers. The first center included 9 patients from the first pedigree and 15 controls (Site 1); the second center included 9 patients from the second pedigree, 1 patient from the third pedigree, and 15 controls (Site 2). Two large pedigrees have been reported before9, 22; the genogram of the third pedigree is shown in Figure S1. The genetic background of BAFME in three pedigrees is homogeneous. Our previous study offered evidence of intronic pentanucleotide expansions in SAMD12 for the first pedigree.22 Using long-read whole-genome sequencing for two patients (patient 12, VI 4, male, 51-years-old; and patient 16, VI 21, female, 41-years-old) from two branches of the second pedigree and one patient (patient 19) from the third pedigree, expanded intronic TTTCA and TTTTA repeat in SAMD12 were also identified (Figure S2 and Table S2). All the participants were right-handed, native Chinese speakers. Patients were diagnosed with BAFME by epileptologists at Xiangya Hospital, Central South University. The clinical and electrophysiological findings met the criteria for the diagnosis of BAFME.1 The Unified Myoclonus Rating Scale (UMRS) and Fahn-Tolosa-Marin Tremor Rating Scale (FTRS) were used to assess the severity of myoclonus and tremor by two experienced neurologists. Electroencephalography and electromyography were performed in all patients. Giant SEPs were assessed when the P25-N33 amplitude was >8.5 μV.23 All healthy controls were recruited via local recruitment advertisements in the hospital and neighboring communities. Exclusion criteria for all participants included acute physical illness, substance abuse or dependence, history of head injury resulting in loss of consciousness, and a major psychiatric or neurologic illness other than BAFME.

This study was approved by the ethics committee of Xiangya Hospital, Central South University, and written informed consent was obtained from all the participants.

Image acquisition

The two sub-data sets were acquired on a 3.0-T Siemens Trio scanner (Site 1) and 3.0-T GE Signal HDx scanner (Site 2), respectively. DTI was obtained using a single-shot echo-planar imaging sequence. Site 1: Repetition time (TR) = 12 000 ms, echo time (TE) = 75 ms, flip angle (FA) = 90 degrees, number of slices = 55, slice thickness = 3 mm, field of view (FOV) = 256 × 256 mm2, matrix = 256 × 256, voxel size = 1 × 1 × 3 mm3, b-value = 1000 s/mm2, 32 noncollinear gradient directions; Site 2: TR = 6500 ms, TE = 93 ms, FA = 30 degrees, number of slices = 50, slice thickness = 3 mm, FOV = 230 × 230 mm2, matrix = 128 × 128, voxel size = 1.8 × 1.8 × 3 mm3, b-value = 1000 s/mm2, 30 noncollinear gradient directions.

High-resolution three-dimensional (3D) brain anatomic images were acquired using a T1-weighted magnetization-prepared rapid gradient echo sequence. Site 1: TR = 7792 ms, TE = 2.98 ms, FOV = 256 × 256 mm2, matrix = 256 × 256, number of slices = 188, slice thickness = 1 mm, FA = 7 degrees, voxel size = 1 × 1 × 1 mm3; Site 2: TR = 1900 ms, TE = 3.41 ms, FOV = 240 × 240 mm2, matrix = 256 × 256, number of slices = 160, slice thickness = 1 mm, FA = 9 degrees, voxel size = 0.9 × 0.9 × 1 mm3.

Cortical morphologic analysis

A completely automated cortical segmentation and parcellation was performed using the standard FreeSurfer version 6.0 pipeline (https://surfer.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu/). The procedure included intensity bias correction, skull stripping, tissue classification, triangular tessellation, image segmentation, surface reconstruction, and quality control. Regions of interest (ROIs) for the motor cortex included the bilateral M1, primary sensory cortex (S1), supplementary motor area (SMA), and premotor cortex (PMC). These gyri were chosen because they actively drive cortical myoclonus or participate in the oscillatory network.24 Three morphometric measures were extracted for each ROI: average cortical thickness, surface area, and volume.

Cerebellum volume analysis

Optimized voxel-based morphometry of infratentorial structures was performed using a spatially unbiased infratentorial template (SUIT) toolbox (http://www.diedrichsenlab.org/imaging/suit.htm). Briefly, it included cerebellum and brainstem isolation, manual corrections, normalization to the SUIT template, Jacobian modulation, and smoothing (full-width half maximum [FWHM] = 4 mm).10, 25 Voxel-based morphometry analysis was performed to test cerebellar gray matter atrophy. The total intracranial volume was used as a covariate and was calculated from T1-weighted images as the sum of the modulated nonlinearly warped gray matter, white matter, and cerebrospinal fluid volumes using the CAT12 toolbox (http://www.neuro.uni-jena.de/cat/).

DTI preprocessing and cerebellar dentate analysis

DTI scans were preprocessed and analyzed using the FMRIB's Diffusion Toolbox in the FSL software (https://fsl.fmrib.ox.ac.uk/fsl/fslwiki/). First, the original data were corrected for distortions caused by the head motion and eddy currents. Fractional anisotropy (FA), axial diffusivity (AD), mean diffusivity (MD), and radial diffusivity (RD) whole-brain maps for each DTI image were generated using DTIFIT. The mean diffusion values were calculated for the bilateral cerebellar dentate ROIs.26

Fiber tracking

Probabilistic tractography was performed using the FSL ProbtrackX program. The parameters were as follows: number of samples = 5000, curvature-threshold = 0.2, step length = 0.5 mm, and subsidiary fiber volume fraction threshold = 0.01.15 The average diffusion value for each tract was extracted. The thalamic center of gravity was calculated using a weighted-average location to estimate the locus of maximum connectivity of each tract.

Dentato-thalamo-M1 tract

We performed probabilistic tractography of both decussating and nondecussating fibers for each patient.15, 27 Segmented FreeSurfer labels were used to generate M1 and thalamic ROIs. Briefly, decussating fibers were generated from the dentate seed to the contralateral M1 target using the contralateral thalamic mask as the waypoint and ipsilateral thalamus as the exclusion mask. The nondecussating fibers were generated from the dentate seed to the ipsilateral M1 using the ipsilateral thalamic mask as the waypoint and the contralateral thalamus as the exclusion mask.

GPi-thalamic tract

GPi mask was obtained from the standard probabilistic BG atlas and transformed into individual diffusion space.28 To refine the GPi mask, the individual whole pallidum FreeSurfer mask was used as a constraint to define boundaries. Probabilistic tractography was initiated from the GPi to the thalamus in each patient and hemisphere.

Statistical analysis

Voxel-based cerebellar analyses were performed between the two groups on volume maps using Statistical Parametric Mapping software, version 12 (https://www.fil.ion.ucl.ac.uk/spm/software/), with site and total intracranial volume as covariates. Cerebellar volume was corrected using a cerebellar gray matter mask. The thresholds were false discovery rate (FDR) corrected to q < .05 at the voxel level and cluster size ≥10 voxels.

ROI-based analyses were performed using two-sided t tests, with the site of the scan as a covariate in the Statistical Package for Social Science (IBM SPSS Statistics 24). The ROI values (thickness, surface area, and volume) of the cortical morphometric measures were divided by the cortical average before analysis. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure was used to control the FDR. FDRs q < .05 were interpreted as statistically significant.

Correlation analysis

Correlation analyses were performed between abnormal imaging values and clinical variables, with the site as the covariate. FDRs q < .05 were considered statistically significant. The variables were as follows: (1) altered dentato-thalamo-M1 tracts and SEP P25-N33 amplitude and (2) abnormal imaging values and disease duration.

Sensitivity analyses

To address the effect of antiseizure medication, we conducted sensitivity analyses in which patients taking antiseizure medication were excluded.

To exclude the scanner effects, despite adopting the most basic and common method that adds site-covariates as controls in group-level analysis,29, 30 we conducted ComBat analysis,31 a developing method, for group comparisons.

In addition, myoclonic jerk is also a symptom of other neurological disorders. We included patients with juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (JME) and matched healthy controls to investigate the specificity of the structural changes observed in BAFME (see Supporting Information Part 7).

3 RESULTS

Demographics and clinical assessments

Detailed demographic information and clinical characteristics were summarized in Table S1. There were no significant differences in gender (p = .59, chi-square test) and age (p = .68, two-sample t test) between patient and control groups. All patients showed epileptiform waves in frontotemporal or frontoparietal regions on electroencephalography and giant SEPs on electromyography. Tremulous movement was found in all BAFME1 patients. Three patients had only tremors; eight patients had both tremors and myoclonus; three patients manifested both tremors and infrequent generalized tonic–clonic seizures (GTCS); and five had tremors, myoclonus, and occasional GTCS. Cerebellar signs (i.e., ataxia, nystagmus, dysmetria, and dysarthria) were not apparent. Seven patients took antiseizure medication (valproic acid for one and phenobarbital for the other six patients). The FTRS and UMRS scores in BAFME patients were 19.5 ± 15.5 and 15.7 ± 18.9, respectively.

Cortical gray matter morphologic results

Relative to healthy controls, we did not observe significant alterations in cortical gray matter morphology in patients with BAFME1. Only slightly reduced cortical volume in the right M1 and left S1 and reduced thickness in the right SMA without multiple-testing correction were detected (Table S3).

Cerebellar volume and dentate nuclei changes

SUIT analysis revealed gray matter loss in bilateral lobules VIIb, VIIIa, VIIIb, and crus II in BAFME1 (FDR correction q < .05, cluster size ≥10 voxels) (Table 1).

Tractography data showed an overlap of the tracts of interest within the ventral thalamus (Table S5). The nondecussating dentato-thalamo-M1 tracts were more medial within the thalamus relative to the decussating fibers. The pallido-thalamic tracts lay more anterior to the dentato-thalamo-M1 tracts.

It is suggested that stringent multiple comparison tests might risk losing the power to detect meaningful results.32 FDR control levels in the range of 0.1–0.2 are originally and practically known to be acceptable and have been applied in several neuroimaging studies.32-36 Due to the rarity of BAFME and the generally small sample sizes for neuroimaging analysis, we tend to report these results to avoid missing potential meaningful findings. We remind readers to interpret the results with caution. In addition, future studies are needed to collect multicenter neuroimaging data to confirm these results.

Correlation between clinical assessments and changed imaging measures

AD values of the bilateral dentate nucleus (left: R = −0.68, p = .002, FDR q = .005; right: R = −0.69, p = .002, FDR q = .003) were correlated significantly with the duration of BAFME (Figure 2A). The left SEP P25-N33 amplitude was correlated significantly with the FA value of the left dentate-left thalamus-left M1 tract (R = −0.66, p = .004, FDR q = .035) and showed a correlated trend with the AD value of the right dentate-left thalamus-left M1 tract (R = −0.61, p = .013, FDR q = .057) (Figure 2B).

Sensitivity analyses

We performed a sensitivity analysis in untreated patients to exclude the effects of antiseizure medications. The results were approximately the same for the cerebellum (exploratory threshold p < .005 uncorrected, cluster size ≥10 voxels) and fiber projections (p < .05) (Tables S6 and S7).

We observed that different harmonization methods did not affect the main results of our findings. We have described the main results of tracts between all patients and controls after ComBat harmonization in the Supporting Information Part 8.

For patients with JME, no significant differences in the cerebellum and cerebellar-thalamo-M1 tracts were observed relative to healthy controls. JME patients showed significantly lower MD in the left GPi-thalamic projection (p = .001, uncorrected; FDR q = .029) and a trend of lower RD in bilateral GPi-thalamic projections (left: p = .005, uncorrected; FDR q = .058; right: p = .011, uncorrected; FDR q = .086) (Figure S3 and Table S9).

4 DISCUSSION

In the current study, we observed abnormalities within cerebellar structures instead of the cerebral cortex in patients with BAFME1. In addition, these patients showed abnormal dentato-thalamo-M1 fibers, which were correlated with SEP P25-N33 amplitude and were independent of disease duration and antiseizure medication, suggesting that this loop might be the underlying cause of cortical tremor. Furthermore, this study revealed altered GPi-thalamic tracts, which may play a regulatory role (Figure 3).

The role of the cerebellum in BAFME1

We observed decreased gray matter volume in the cerebellum, including lobules VII, VIII, and curs II. According to the double motor (lobules I-VI and VIII) and triple non-motor (lobules VI/crus I, crus II/VIIB, and IX/X) representation,37 gray matter loss in BAFME1 represents both motor and non-motor processing dysfunctions.

Consistent with our findings, previous studies revealed that cerebellar motor area changes in BAFME1 are predominantly in lobule VIII.6, 10 Recent literature suggests that lesions of different cerebellar motor representations produce different motor deficits.38 Unlike the first cerebellar motor representation (lobules I-VI), the second motor representation (VIII) might correspond to a less extreme level of pure motor information processing and produce little motor deficit,37 which might explain why cerebellar signs are not apparent in BAFME1 pedigrees. In addition, we observed cerebellar atrophy in areas of triple non-motor representations, which is generally consistent with the previous study.10 Our previous resting state functional MRI (rsfMRI) research found disrupted functional connectivity between these non-motor regions and a wide range of cortical areas within default and control networks, indicating that structural changes might explain cognitive impairments.9

It has been suggested that cerebellar involvement differs among the BAFME subtypes. For example, compared to BAFME3, patients with BAFME1 had more subtle cerebellar abnormalities. Neuropathological investigations revealed inconspicuous cerebellar lesions in BAFME1 patients with heterozygous mutations,3 whereas patients with BAFME3 showed cerebellar degeneration.4 In addition, neuroimaging findings suggested that cerebellar atrophy in BAFME3 patients included more widespread motor (lobule V, VI, and VIII) and non-motor (crus I, lobule IX and X) areas than in BAFME1 patients.6, 10, 11 The distinct degree of cerebellar damage might be related to noncoding repeat expansions in different genes. Further neuroimaging studies in different genotypes of BAFME will be helpful to confirm this and elucidate the cerebellar involvement specific to BAFME subtypes.

In addition to gray matter atrophy, we observed a trend of reduced AD in the bilateral dentate nuclei. Pathological features of the dentate nucleus include loss of neuronal cells, reactive gliosis,4, 39 and myelin pallor in the hilus,40 which might affect axonal structures and reflect differences in AD. The diffusion values of bilateral dentate nuclei are related to disease duration, which is consistent with previous electrophysiological studies, suggesting that BAFME is a mild progressive disorder.41-43 However, we observed no dentate diffusional alterations in untreated patients. Further longitudinal imaging studies are needed to validate the results and determine whether cerebellar abnormalities are primary or secondary changes.

In the present study, the morphological results of the motor cortex showed only a decreased tendency, which should be interpreted with caution. The asymmetric structural pattern is consistent with previous research (including asymmetric cortical metabolic, functional, and structural alterations).6, 10, 12, 19, 44 However, because of the relatively small sample sizes and low statistical power, the results did not provide sufficient evidence to suggest cerebral morphological changes in patients with BAFME1. This is consistent with previous pathology findings showing limited involvement of the sensorimotor cortex,3 which further supported that cortical hyperactivity might result from the failure of cerebello-cortical control. Future multicenter studies including larger samples are needed to verify the results. Specifically, longitudinal studies can be used to characterize the changes during disease progression and approximate the true longitudinal trajectories of BAFME.

Cerebello-cortical projection might be the underlying pathogenesis of cortical tremor in BAFME1

We observed decreased AD and FA in the dentato-thalamo-M1 projections, which were independent of disease duration and antiseizure medication. FA is thought to reflect the fiber density, axonal diameter, and myelination in the white matter. Often, disorganization of the axon fiber architecture leads to unrestricted or isotropic diffusion and, as a result, reduced FA values.45 AD is the amount of apparent diffusion along the long axis of the axonal tract. Decreased AD and FA might reflect white matter damage, and lower AD might be attributed to axonal damage, which creates barriers that hinder water diffusion (e.g., cellular debris and microtubule disarrangement).46

In addition, we also performed correlation analyses to interpret the biological meaning of cerebellar efferent tracts. Giant SEP P25-N33 amplitude, a biomarker of cortical hyperexcitability and the feature of cortical tremor/myoclonus, is the typical finding in BAFME.23 The precise generator source of P25-N33 amplitude has not been fully elucidated; however, the involvement of the M1 has been suggested.47-49 In the present study, we found that diffusion measures of cerebellar-thalamo-M1 were negatively correlated with SEP P25-N33 amplitude, suggesting that lower diffusion values, that is, more severe microstructural damage, were correlated with higher cortical hyperexcitability. Our results expanded the observation of Wang et al. on the relationship between the cerebellar white matter volume and SEP P25-N33 amplitude in BAFME1.6 In addition, observations of previous functional research could complement the present structural findings. For example, Dubbioso et al. observed increased facilitation and decreased inhibition in the sensorimotor cortex, as well as functional impairment of thalamo-cortical connection in patients with BAFME2 by transcranial magnetic stimulation paradigms and ad hoc SEP protocols.43 Using resting-state fMRI, Wang et al. investigated increased ultra-low-frequency oscillations in left cerebellar lobule VIII, and decreased higher-frequency ones in the left precentral gyrus in BAFME1 patients, indicating that poor inhibitory output from the cerebellum may lead to increased cortical hyperexcitability.10 Therefore, together with previous findings, our results could further support the pathophysiological hypothesis of cerebellar-thalamo-cortical network abnormality underlying BAFME.

GPi-thalamic projections might play a regulatory role in BAFME1

Our study revealed increased FA, decreased MD, and decreased RD in the GPi-thalamic projections in BAFME1 patients. Higher FA values indicated a larger number of similarly aligned neurons. Decreased RD may be due to the thickening of the myelin sheath, leading to reduced perpendicular water diffusion. MD assesses the directionally averaged diffusion magnitude; thus decreased MD indicates increased diffusion restriction.50 Therefore, our findings could be explained by an increased cellular density with increased fiber coherence in the GPi-thalamic projection of patients with BAFME1. Current evidence suggests that the dentate nuclei are linked to the indirect basal ganglia pathway, which conveys suppressive information relevant to the termination of the action.51 Our previous rsfMRI research revealed enhanced functional connectivity between the cerebellum and putamen.9 Therefore, basal ganglia changes might serve a regulatory role. Future longitudinal studies are encouraged to investigate the detailed structural and functional basis of the cerebellar-basal ganglia network and open avenues for interventions in BAFME.

Patients with JME showed significantly changed GPi-thalamic projections compared to matched controls. However, no evidence supported the alteration in cerebellum and cerebellar-thalamo-M1 tracts in JME. As for BAFME in the present study, apart from GPi-thalamic tracts, we also observed abnormal diffusion values of dentato-thalamo-M1 tracts and found significant relationships between the tracts and cortical hyperexcitability. Therefore, although BAFME and JME both have myoclonic jerks, there are some differences in neuroimaging changes. Previous studies also reported the distinctions. For example, the thalamo-SMA connection and basal ganglion system are commonly affected structures in JME,52-54 whereas alterations of BAFME mainly involve the cerebellum and cerebellar-cortical connectivity.1, 9-11 So we think that the dentato-thalamo-M1 projection is the foundation of cortical tremor/myoclonus in BAFME and might be considered as disease specific, which is different from JME, whereas the GPi-thalamic projection might be also involved in other diseases with myoclonic jerks.

5 LIMITATIONS

One of the limitations of the present study is the lack of behavioral data related to the cerebellar-thalamo-cortical loop. Future studies are needed to conduct cerebellar-thalamo-cortical loop–dependent tasks such as the visuomotor adaptation task to verify the role of the cerebellar-cortical circuit in BAFME. In addition, other functional avenues are needed to explore the pathophysiology of the cortical tremor, which could be assessed using fMRI and a number of neurophysiological techniques such as noninvasive brain stimulation techniques. Besides, it is valuable to explore the involvement of cerebellar and basal ganglion networks in other diseases with similar symptoms, such as essential tremor.

6 CONCLUSION

This study revealed abnormal structural features of BAFME1 within the cerebellar-motor and basal ganglia loop. Our work supported the hypothesis that the cerebello-thalamo-cortical loop might be the structural basis of the cortical tremor in BAFME1, where the cerebellum might play a role in its origin. We also suggested that basal ganglia participation might act as a regulatory mechanism. Our findings provide new insight into the neuroanatomic substrate of BAFME.
